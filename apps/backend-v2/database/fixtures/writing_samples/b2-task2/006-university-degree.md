The debate over whether a university degree or practical experience is more important for career success has been ongoing for many years. While both paths have their merits, I believe the ideal approach depends on the specific profession.

On the one hand, a university degree is often considered essential for many careers. Professions such as medicine, law, and engineering require extensive theoretical knowledge that can only be obtained through formal education. A degree also serves as a credential that demonstrates to employers that a candidate has the discipline and intelligence to complete a challenging program. Moreover, universities provide networking opportunities that can be valuable throughout one's career.

On the other hand, practical experience and skills are becoming increasingly valued in today's job market. Many technology companies, for example, now hire candidates based on their portfolio of work rather than their educational background. A software developer who has built real applications may be more attractive to employers than a graduate who only has theoretical knowledge. Furthermore, vocational training and apprenticeships offer a faster and often more affordable path to employment.

In my opinion, both education and experience play important but different roles. While a degree provides a solid foundation of knowledge, practical experience allows individuals to apply that knowledge in real-world situations. The most successful professionals are those who combine both — they pursue formal education while also seeking internships and hands-on opportunities. I believe that in the future, the distinction between education and experience will become less important as employers increasingly focus on what candidates can actually do rather than what certificates they hold.
